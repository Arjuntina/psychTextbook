7.1 Biological Rhythms

  • Biological rhythms are natural variations in biological functions, hormonal activity, temperature, and sleep that typically cycle every twenty-four to twenty-five hours.

  • 7.1a Circadian Rhythms

    • Both people and animals exhibit circadian rhythms.
    • The brain structure that functions as a biological clock is the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN), and it prepares for our daily period of activity by anticipatory rises in heart rate, body temperature, and certain hormone levels.

7.2 The Science of Sleep and Dreaming

  • Sleep is the natural, periodically occurring state of rest characterized by reduced activity, lessened responsiveness to stimuli, and distinctive patterns of brain activity.

  • 7.2a Stages of Sleep

    • There are periods of sleep when the eyes move rapidly and periods with little or no eye movement, called REM and NREM, respectively.
    • REM sleep is a state of sleep characterized by rapid eye movements and often associated with dreaming.
    • In contrast, NREM sleep is the stages of sleep during which rapid eye movements typically do not occur. (Dreaming occurs far less frequently during NREM sleep than during REM sleep.)
    • Muscular movement is inhibited during REM sleep by activity in a network of cells called the pontine reticular formation, located in the pons of the brain. When it’s active, the body experiences profound loss of muscle tone, making it almost impossible to move.
    • REM behavior disorder occurs when a person thrashes about violently, leaps out of bed, and may even attack their bed partners due to not being paralyzed during REM as most people are.
    • There are four distinct stages of sleep:
      • Stage 1 – Light sleep that occurs just after dozing off, characterized by brain waves called theta waves.
      • Stage 2 – Stage of sleep that typically follows Stage 1 sleep, characterized by brief bursts of brain activity called sleep spindles as well as K-complex responses to stimuli such as noises.
      • Stage 3 – Stage of sleep that typically follows Stage 2 sleep, characterized by an EEG tracing, 20 to 50 percent of which consists of delta waves and virtually no eye movements.
      • Stage 4 – Deepest level of sleep, characterized by an EEG tracing exceeding 50 percent delta waves and virtually no eye movements.

  • 7.2b The Sleep Cycle

    • It takes about 45 minutes to reach Stage 4 after first falling asleep, and then we stay in it for about 30 to 40 minutes, before returning to Stage 3 then 2 and 1.
    • The first period of REM occurs when we reenter Stage 1.
    • Throughout the night, we move through successive cycles, drifting up and down between the various phases of REM and NREM sleep.
    • The cycles last about 90 minutes, and we complete five during the night.

  • 7.2c Change in Sleep Patterns with Age

    • The percentage of the night’s sleep that is spent in REM state decreases throughout life.
    • The amount of time spent in Stage 4 sleep also changes with age.
    • Older people waken more easily.

  • 7.2d Brain Mechanisms of Sleep

    • The following are major brain structures involved in sleep and wakefulness:
      • Reticular activating system (RAS) – Set of neural circuits extending from the lower brain up to the thalamus that plays a critical role in controlling arousal and alertness.
      • Raphe nucleus – A group of serotonin-containing neurons extending from the raphe nuclei, located in the pons and medulla throughout the limbic system and forebrain.
      • Acetylcholine activates cortical structures that are essential to ongoing behavior and thought processes.
      • Serotonin and adenosine are the neurotransmitters of sleep.
      • Adenosine accumulates during wakefulness.
      • Caffeine keeps you alert by blocking adenosine receptors.
      • Melatonin – A hormone produced by the pineal gland that regulates circadian rhythms.

  • 7.2e The Function of Sleep

    • Studies show that sleep deprivation in animals is fatal, and the effect is believed to be the same in humans.

  • 7.2f Theories of Sleep Function

    • The following functions of sleep have been proposed:
      • Conserving energy and thus preventing exhaustion.
      • Avoiding predation, such that we are prohibited from interacting with the environment at times when we are not physiologically suited to function adaptively.
      • Sleep restores resources that are depleted in our daily activities.
      • Sleep is essential for brain homeostasis.
      • Information learned prior to sleep is better remembered than if sleep did not occur, and that performance is better if sleep follows a learning task.
      • Sleep deprivation causes impaired memory formation.

  • 7.2g Dreaming

    • In a study by Dement (1960), sleep-deprived subjects demonstrated a number of effects not shown by a control group, including irritability, anxiety, hostility, aggression, and they had trouble concentrating on tasks. They also showed signs of being REM starved, entering REM sleep almost as soon as they dozed off.
    • Other effects of REM deprivation include death, deteriorated appearance, skin lesions, weight loss, increased energy expenditure, and decreased body temperature.
    • REM sleep has been observed and recorded in all animals studied.
    • According to Freud, symbolic processes that represent wishes or desires that become fulfilled by the dream code are the contents of a dream.
    • Dreams are disguised expressions of wishes that have been repressed.
      • Manifest content – the disguised version of the latent content, or true meaning, of dreams
      • Latent content – the hidden content or true meaning of dreams

7.3 Disorders of Sleep

  • Sleep disorders are a class of disorders that interfere with sleep, including apnea, terrors, nightmares, and sleepwalking.

  • 7.3a Insomnia

    • Sleep disorder characterized by a consistent inability to get to sleep or by frequent awakening during sleep.
    • May be temporary or situational, such as related to stress, or chronic and associated with anxiety or depression.
      • More individuals experienced insomnia during the COVID pandemic.
      • Instructor: Ask students about their experiences with sleep during the lockdowns.

  • 7.3b Sleep Apnea

    • Sleep disorder characterized by irregular breathing during sleep.
    • Seems to occur when the brain stops sending signals that trigger the breathing response.

  • 7.3c Narcolepsy

    • Sleep disorder characterized by falling asleep suddenly and uncontrollably.

  • 7.3d Nightmares

    • Bad dream that occurs during REM sleep
    • Sleep terrors are a sleep disorder in which a person suddenly awakens from Stage 4 sleep in panic, typically with no recollection of a bad dream.

  • 7.3e Sleepwalking

    • Sleep disorder, characterized by walking in one’s sleep during Stage 3 or 4 of NREM sleep; also known as somnambulism.
    • Sleepwalking may subject a person to extreme danger.

  • 7.3f Sleep Talking

    • The production of speech or speech sounds associated with sleep without subjective awareness; also referred to as somniloquy.
    • Sleep walkers rarely reveal secrets.

7.4 Hypnosis

  • State of altered consciousness characterized by a deep relaxation and detachment, as well as heightened suggestibility to the hypnotist’s directives.

  • 7.4a Phenomena Associated with Hypnosis

    • Athletic ability – Can act as a powerful motivator, providing the extra impetus to close the gap between potential and actual performance.
    • Relief of physical ailments – Relieves the symptoms of stress-related illnesses, including asthma, ulcers, and colitis.
    • Pain relief – Can reduce the pain associated with childbirth, back problems, arthritis, dental procedures, burns, and even major surgery
    • Memory enhancement – No reliable evidence that hypnosis enhances either distant or recent memories, and evidence obtained while in a hypnotic state is not admissible in court.
    • Posthypnotic suggestion – Suggestion or instruction to a hypnotized person that motivates that person to perform an action or actions after returning to a normal state of consciousness.

  • 7.4b Explaining Hypnosis

    • Ernest Hilgard offered dissociation theory, which says our behaviors become separated from or dissociated from our awareness.
    • Another theory says that there are several levels of behavioral control: conscious, executive control, and lower levels of control that can be directly activated by hypnosis.
    • Barber (1977) said all the phenomena associated with hypnosis can be demonstrated in people who are not hypnotized.
    • Hypnotic suggestibility is the predisposition to attend to a hypnotist’s suggestions.

7.5 Consciousness

  • A sense of self and the world around us.
  • Neuroscientists believe that consciousness evolved from more primitive states of awareness that allowed animals to more efficiently interact with their environments.

  • 7.5a Neurobiology of Consciousness

    • Sensory neglect is a lack of awareness of or attention to stimuli on one side of the body when damage has occurred to the opposite-side parietal lobe. 
    • We tend to assume that our actions are free or not caused by antecedent conditions.
    • Free will is the assertion that our choices and actions are not caused by antecedent events, but emerge spontaneously from the mind.
    • Research shows that the notion of free will is an illusion.
